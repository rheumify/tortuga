About Us

Why this exists

I love animals — like, genuinely obsessed — and I built Tortuga to get more people just as hooked on how wild and weird the natural world really is.

The mission

Spark curiosity

Every animal on Earth is doing something incredible right now — surviving, building, outsmarting, adapting. Tortuga is here to share those facts in a way that actually makes you go "wait, WHAT?" and want to learn more.

The why

The more you know, the more you care

People protect what they love, and they love what they understand. If learning that octopuses have three hearts or that sloths poop once a week makes you smile, maybe it also makes you want to keep their world safe.

The vibe

No boring textbook energy

This isn't a homework assignment. It's a field guide built for curious people who want the fun, jaw-dropping, true stuff — bite-sized, honest, and a little bit chaotic. Pick an animal from the sidebar and dive in.

Field notes · Our one planet

Save the Planet

Every animal in this guide shares one home with us. The cool news: small things you do actually move the needle. Here's the real talk — no doom, just facts and moves you can pull off this week.

🔥 Global warming & the animals

Melting ice

Polar animals are losing the floor

As the planet warms, sea ice melts earlier each year. Polar bears, penguins, seals and walruses use that ice to hunt, rest and raise babies — less ice means way less room to live.

Coral

Hot water bleaches reefs

When the ocean gets too warm, coral kicks out the tiny algae that feed it and turns ghostly white. Reefs are home to about 25% of ALL ocean species, so a bleached reef is like a whole apartment block going dark.

Turtles

Heat is skewing sea turtle babies

Remember how nest temperature picks a turtle's sex? Hotter sand makes mostly females. On some beaches it's now over 99% female hatchlings — which is a real problem for finding mates later.

Moving home

Animals are packing up and leaving

As habitats heat up, animals migrate to cooler areas or higher mountains to survive. Some can move fast enough — others run out of "up" or "north" and have nowhere left to go.

🗑️ Plastic & littering

The mix-up

Animals mistake trash for food

Sea turtles see a floating plastic bag and think "jellyfish — yum." Birds feed bottle caps and bits of plastic to their chicks. It fills their stomachs with stuff they can't digest.

Forever trash

Plastic basically never leaves

A plastic bottle can take around 450 years to break down. It doesn't really vanish — it just shreds into tiny "microplastics" that end up in fish, water, and yep, even us.

Tangled

Ghost gear & six-pack rings

Lost fishing nets and loose plastic rings trap seals, dolphins, turtles and fish, making it hard for them to swim, eat, or escape predators. Snipping plastic rings before tossing them genuinely helps.

Truckloads

A garbage truck a minute

Roughly one garbage truck's worth of plastic enters the ocean every single minute. Litter on a street near you can wash into a storm drain and end up in the sea — it's all connected.

💪 What YOU can actually do

Reusable

Ditch single-use plastic

Carry a refillable water bottle and reusable bag. One reusable bottle can replace hundreds of plastic ones a year. Easy win, low effort, big payoff.

Sort it

Recycle right, not just often

Rinse containers and check what your area actually accepts. Tossing dirty or wrong items in the recycling can ruin a whole batch — so doing it correctly matters more than doing a lot.

Cleanup

Pick up 3 pieces of litter

The "take 3 for the sea" idea: grab three pieces of trash whenever you leave a beach, park or trail. If everyone did it, that's millions of pieces gone. Group cleanups level it up.

Power down

Save energy at home

Lights off when you leave, unplug chargers, walk or bike for short trips. Less wasted energy means fewer emissions warming the planet — and a lower bill, which your parents will love.

Speak up

Tell people what you learned

The biggest move of all: share the facts. People protect what they understand. Show a friend the turtle fact, post it, talk about it — caring spreads faster than litter.

Backyard

Make a tiny wild spot

Plant a flower for the bees, leave out fresh water for birds, skip the bug spray on a corner of the yard. Little safe zones add up to a friendlier neighborhood for wildlife.
